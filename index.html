Real History Project First Draft

Research Question

What social, political, and religious factors contributed to the persecution of Mormons in the eastern United States, and how did these pressures lead to their migration and eventual settlement in Utah?

U.S. History Themes

Migration and Settlement (MIG)

My project directly addresses the Mormon migration westward, examining how religious persecution and social pressures forced the Mormons to leave the eastern United States and seek refuge in Utah. I will explore why the Mormons chose to migrate, how they adapted to new physical and social environments in Utah, and how their settlement shaped the region, overall analyzing the push and pull factors behind their migration and the transformation of Utah as a result of their settlement.

Politics and Power (PCE)

The political conflicts that led to Mormon persecution, particularly how state and local governments viewed the Mormon Church’s influence and practices, relate to this theme. I will explore how the power struggles between the Mormons and their neighbors, and between the Mormons and local governments, influenced their decision to migrate. Additionally, I will discuss how the Utah War, which occurred after the Mormon Migration, highlights the political tensions and power struggles between the federal government and the Mormon settlers over governance in the Utah Territory.

Summary of Narrative

The persecution of the Mormons in the eastern United States was largely due to a combination of social, political, and religious factors. Socially, the rapid growth of the Mormon community and their different lifestyle led to tensions with non-Mormons. Their religious beliefs and practices, particularly polygamy (the practice of having multiple wives), were considered too radical, which made them a target for religious intolerance, especially during the Christian revivals like the Second Great Awakening. Politically, conflicts arose as the Mormons sought autonomy (the right or condition of self-government), culminating in violence such as Missouri Executive Order 44, which called for their extermination. The political power they amassed in places like Nauvoo, Illinois also led to fears of a theocracy. These tensions ultimately escalated into armed conflicts and the murder of Joseph Smith, founder and leader of the Latter-Day Saint movement, and his brother, forcing the Mormons to migrate.

Under Brigham Young's leadership, the Mormons embarked on a westward migration along the Mormon Trail, seeking refuge in the Great Basin, which lay outside of the U.S.’ borders at the time. Their journey was marked by hardships, including harsh weather and inadequate supplies, but they established settlements along the way and eventually reached the Salt Lake Valley in 1847. There, they built a prosperous community despite continuing political struggles and tensions with the federal government, which eventually ended with the Utah War. The migration is a significant example of the themes of religious persecution, migration, and the struggle for political autonomy.
