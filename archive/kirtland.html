The Mormon Exodus from Kirtland, Ohio

Source

    The expulsion of the Mormons from Kirtland, Ohio, in the late 1830s resulted from internal strife within the Mormon community and growing external pressures. After the completion of the Kirtland Temple in 1836, the city experienced a period of rapid economic expansion as new members arrived, creating heightened demand for land, housing, and goods. Joseph Smith and other Church leaders sought to address these needs by establishing the Kirtland Safety Society Anti-Banking Company, hoping to convert the Church's land holdings into liquid assets that would benefit the community. However, the society struggled financially and quickly failed, especially in the face of the 1837 National Panic, which triggered bank closures across the country. Many members, financially devastated by the bank's collapse, blamed Joseph Smith and the Church leadership, leading to widespread disillusionment and even apostasy among the Saints (Kettley, Garr, and Manscill, "Zion's Camp").

    The fallout from the Kirtland Safety Society's failure severely strained the Church. The "spirit of speculation" that had permeated the Kirtland community led to distrust, pride, and accusations among the members, and soon even prominent Church leaders joined the wave of dissent. Some leaders began questioning Joseph Smith's authority, and a faction sought to replace him with David Whitmer. As the tension mounted, a violent incident occurred when Warren Parrish, leading a group of apostates, armed themselves with "pistols and bowie-knives" and stormed a Church meeting. This aggressive opposition, combined with mounting legal troubles for Joseph Smith and physical threats to his supporters, forced Smith and other faithful members to flee Kirtland. This mass exodus, beginning in late 1837 and accelerating into 1838, saw the remaining faithful abandon their homes and move west to Missouri, where they hoped to find refuge and rebuild their community.
